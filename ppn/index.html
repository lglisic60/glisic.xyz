A Decentralized Private Payment Network

By Leo Glisic · DRAFT v1, July 2026 · Litepaper (PDF, v1)

This week, a consortium of more than 140 companies, including Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, BlackRock, Coinbase, and Google, announced OUSD, a stablecoin aimed at everyday payments. Zero fees for businesses to mint and redeem. Most of the reserve income shared with the partner companies instead of kept by the issuer. Live on multiple chains later this year. Circle’s stock fell 13% on the news, and one outlet asked whether this is Libra’s second act.

Here’s my honest reaction: they’re right. When the biggest names in money agree that payments are moving on-chain, that debate is over. And that’s the part of the announcement worth taking seriously. Payments are moving on-chain. The only question left is what version we get.

Because there are two questions the announcement doesn’t answer.

First: who can see your payments? OUSD launches on fully public ledgers, so as announced, every purchase is visible to anyone on earth with a block explorer. And the network is run by the companies that process payments today, many of whose businesses run on payment data. Nothing published so far says what the operators will see, or addresses privacy at all. Maybe that’s coming later. But it isn’t there now.

Second: who can turn your account off? Maybe nobody, but I doubt it. The stablecoins OUSD is competing with can freeze any address they choose, and a coin operated by a consortium board is not going to have fewer controls than the coins it replaces. I’m not accusing anyone of bad intent. It’s just how consortiums work. Somebody holds the switch.

So here’s my claim, and I know how it sounds. You can build the other version: decentralized, censorship-resistant, more private than anything in traditional finance, and built to be fully compliant in every country where it operates. All four at once. We’re used to assuming privacy and compliance trade off against each other, but they don’t have to. The rest of this essay is the design. I’m publishing it, along with the full technical paper, for anyone to build.

So how does it work? It’s actually kind of simple.

You pick a Privacy Guardian. It can be a bank, a fintech, a merchant network, a telecom, or (where the law allows it) a decentralized protocol, so no institution is needed at all. Your Guardian is the only party that sees your account: your identity per local rules, your balance, your payments. Everyone else, meaning the public, other users, and other Guardians, sees encrypted data they can’t read. Compare that to a card payment today, which is seen by the merchant’s processor, the acquiring bank, the card network, the issuing bank, and a handful of fraud and data vendors behind them. Here it’s one party, and you chose it.

And your Guardian never touches your money. All funds sit in one neutral smart contract on Ethereum. No Guardian can freeze your funds, and no consortium of Guardians can either. You can exit to Ethereum, or switch Guardians, anytime, with permission from nobody. Like changing phone carriers and keeping your number. Payments confirm in about a second, everything restores from a seed phrase, and payments should be free at the point of use, funded by the yield on reserves (that’s the expectation, anyway).

“Governments will never allow this.” Well, walk through what a government actually loses here: nothing. Warrants and subpoenas work through your Guardian exactly the way they work through a bank today. Each country decides which Guardians can serve its residents, the same way it licenses any other business. And exits happen on the public chain: you can always leave, but the money comes out in the open where everyone can see it. So there’s nothing here a government needs to ban. If it doesn’t like a Guardian, it bans that Guardian.

Okay, but why would it stay honest?

Start with the money. OUSD holds deposits as yield-bearing reserves and shares the yield with its corporate partners. This design runs on the same economics: deposits held as a diversified basket of yield-bearing dollar reserves, balances redeemable one-for-one. The difference is where the yield goes. Say half pays the Guardians for their work, and the other half funds privacy insurance that pays users.

Every Guardian is backed by an insurance pool: the Guardian’s own money (say 15% of the pool), which it loses if it fails its users, plus capital from an open underwriting market where anyone can participate. And the coverage per user is public. You can’t inspect a Guardian’s security practices, but you can see exactly what you’d be paid if your privacy fails, and compare Guardians on that number.

Then there’s my favorite part: the honeypot. Inside each pool sits a standing bounty (say 5% of the pool), paid automatically by smart contract to anyone who can prove they possess a Guardian’s user data. The proof is a zero-knowledge proof, so proving the theft doesn’t leak the data again. And when a claim succeeds, the code does everything at once: the bounty gets paid, the Guardian’s 15% is forfeited, every affected user gets compensated automatically (and privately), and users can rotate to new Guardians so the stolen data goes stale.

Now play out the incentives. The Guardian never steals its own honeypot, since it would lose its bigger stake plus its whole business to win a smaller prize. But an insider can claim it, and that’s intended. It forces the Guardian to run internal controls so hardened that no employee can ever assemble the full dataset. And anyone who buys stolen data can double-cross the seller and claim the bounty themselves, so data thieves can’t trust each other. The entity most paranoid about losing your data becomes your Privacy Guardian. Aligned incentives.

Plus, one more thing the design makes impossible: fractional reserve. Every Guardian’s books are checked against its actual funds, cryptographically, at every settlement. Books that don’t match the money can’t settle.

“But most people don’t care about privacy. They’ll just use the corporate coin.” Yea, they just might. Most of them probably will, and that’s fine. We can’t want more privacy for people than they want for themselves. We can only give them the option. Today, that option doesn’t exist.

So here’s the offer. There’s no token here, and no company. Just a design, published for anyone to build. The corporate version of on-chain payments is coming either way. 140 of the biggest companies in the world just committed to it. I think the open version should exist right beside it, on Ethereum, and that’s why I’m publishing this now instead of sitting on it.

If you want to build it, it’s yours.
